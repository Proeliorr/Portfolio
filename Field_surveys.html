Field Surveys

Field surveys serve as the foundation for numerous projects, particularly in fields like forestry, where the dense canopy can distort the accuracy of modern equipment like GNSS receivers, affecting fixing times and introducing errors in both vertical and horizontal measurements. Additionally, certain tasks necessitate manual data collection as they cannot be accomplished remotely.

Fortunately, technology aids in this data collection process, allowing for the replacement of traditional paper forms and reducing the need for extensive post-survey work.




During my studies, I have become proficient in various commercial data collection software, including those associated with ESRI ArcGIS Collector/Field Maps, as well as open-source (QField) or open-source-derived (Mergin Maps) platforms.



My utilization of the provided software primarily involves preprocessing, verification, and deployment onto the server as user-friendly geoportals, predominantly within the ArcGIS environment. When referring to preprocessing, I mainly focus on generating digital data collection templates, offering an efficient alternative to paper forms. This enables swift offline data storage and cloud streaming, ensuring safer and more interactive data collection processes.




In recent years, various QGIS-based solutions for digitizing field surveys have emerged. One such is an app called Mergin Maps. I tested it using its student license.


In addition to my academic studies, I gained practical experience through summer jobs and undertook several private projects. One notable project was the development of an educational path in the Piwniczna Forestry District in southern Poland. The inspiration for this project stemmed from the introduction of the "Overnight in the Forest" pilot program by the State Forest, the largest forest owner in Poland, which permitted legal overnight stays in designated forest areas under specific safety guidelines.


The educational path aimed to cater
to bushcraft and camping enthusiasts while serving as a potential resource in emergencies. It offered two length options and included additional tourist infrastructure such as spatial coordinates and educational field boards along its route.
